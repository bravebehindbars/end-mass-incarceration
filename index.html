Overcrowding

Correctional facilities are designed to accommodate a specific number of inmates. As their population grows, fresh improvised beds are welded into the walls, converting single cells into doubles, doubles into triples, and so on.

The Sentencing Project

"There are 2 million people in the nation’s prisons and jails—a 500% increase over the last 40 years. Over 770,000 people in U.S. prisons were serving sentences of 10 years or longer in 2019—56% of the total prison population. This is an increase from 587,000 people in 2000, representing 44% of the prison population in that year" - Ghandnoosh et al., 2022

ACLU

Prison Living Conditions

Individuals housed under humane circumstances for incarceration will be more receptive to and capable of participating in rehabilitation programs. On the other side, those who are subjected to harsh punishment and abuse are more likely to return to society emotionally destroyed and in subpar or worse physical and mental health than when they arrived.

The Spreading of Diseases

The facilitation of infectious disease transmission in prisons is facilitated by the presence of environmental variables such as inadequate ventilation, overcrowding, overrepresentation of vulnerable groups, and heightened susceptibility owing to other medical conditions. Prisons can exacerbate infectious diseases, as individuals who are at a higher risk of contracting certain infectious diseases are incarcerated in environments with limited control mechanisms. Influenza, COVID-19, Tuberculosis, Hepatitis B and C, Sexually transmitted infections (STIs), Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Varicella, Ectoparasite infections, and foodborne illnesses are among the most prevalent diseases that are transmitted through prison systems.

Solitary Confinement

It is estimated that 80,000-100,000 individuals in the United States are being held in some form of cell confinement consisting of 22-24 hours a day and minimal interaction Medical study indicates that "isolation syndrome," characterized by symptoms such as anxiety, despair, rage, cognitive difficulties, perceptual distortions, paranoia, psychosis, self-harm, and suicide, can be brought on by depriving an individual of meaningful human interaction. A person's personality and mental health can be destroyed by prolonged isolation, and these consequences might last long after the period of segregation has ended. Solitary confinement should be considered torture or ill-treatment in and of itself, especially if it is lengthy or indefinite. It also raises the possibility that torture or other cruel treatment will go unreported and undiscovered.

Tainted Air and Water

Too many abandoned industrial sites are used as prisons; these locations were only thought suitable for the disposal of hazardous waste. Within three miles of federal Superfund sites—the most seriously polluted locations needing considerable cleanup—lie one-third (32%) of state and federal prisons. Studies advise avoiding residing, working, or attending school in close proximity to Superfund sites because to the association with a shortened life expectancy and a plethora of horrifying ailments. Prisoners are continuously exposed to severe environmental risks, such as contaminated water and dangerous air pollution, because of their location on or near wastelands. These circumstances show up as illnesses and fatalities that are clearly related to those risks.

Recidivism

The term "recidivism" denotes the reoccurrence of criminal conduct by an individual, typically following the imposition of penalties or the implementation of intervention for a prior offense.

Substance Abuse Programs

According to a recent evaluation in Minnesota, participation in substance use disorder programs can reduce recidivism by 23%

Education

The more education a person receives, the less likely they are to be re-arrested or re-imprisoned. Studies have shown that education programs can lower recidivism rates by 43%, and can also increase the chances of finding employment after release by 13%.

Community Based Programs

These programs can reduce recidivism by up to 22% and provide youth with the tools to grow, such as academic support, job training, community ties, and healthcare.

Frequently Asked Questions

What type of medical care do prisoners receive?

The availability of medical and dental services in a penitentiary is severely restricted. In general, each prison has a clinic or infirmary to provide medical care for ill inmates. However, the majority of prisons do not permit sick inmates to "go to medical" unless they submit a written request and receive a "lay-in" notice granting permission. Receiving a lay-in may take 3-5 days, at which time the sick prisoner must just find a way to get by. Medical professionals typically operate in inadequate environments and receive inferior compensation than their colleagues in the external world and Prescription shortages virtually always occur. Dental are tends to be reactive rather than preventative. It can take years to see a dentist just for an initial x-ray and assessment.

What are the effects of incarceration?

Incarceration can lead to recidivism, with more than half of people being incarcerated again within three years of their release. A person's ability to reintegrate into society may also be hampered, leading to issues with housing, work, family, and health. Not only may overcrowding exacerbate mental health issues, but it can also lead to an increase in aggression, self-harm, and suicide.

What increased the jail and prison population?

  • The "War on Drugs" sentencing guidelines, which included mandatory minimum prison sentences and, in certain jurisdictions, a three-strikes statute, were implemented in the 1970s and led to a sharp increase in the number of people incarcerated for drug offenses.
  • Pre-trial detention has caused overcrowding in America's jails. Many people cannot afford the bail given to secure their release until trial and therefore remain in jail.
  • Minor offenses such as tickets or unpaid fines has been a cause for overcrowding in our jail system.
  • Supervised release violations.
